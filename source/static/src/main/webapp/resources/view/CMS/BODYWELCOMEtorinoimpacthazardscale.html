Torino Impact Hazard Scale 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

This browser explorer places three real near-Earth-object cases on the IAU Torino Impact Hazard Scale, then shows an approaching asteroid's beacon change color to match each case's actual assigned band.

The Torino Impact Hazard Scale is a 0-10 integer scale developed by Richard Binzel (MIT), adopted by the International Astronomical Union in 1999 and revised in 2005. It combines impact probability and expected consequence into one number with five color bands: 0 White (no hazard), 1 Green (a normal event with no unusual danger), 2-4 Yellow (merits attention by astronomers), 5-7 Orange (a threatening event), and 8-10 Red (a certain collision). Asteroid 99942 Apophis carries the highest Torino rating ever assigned to a real object: on 2004-12-27 it was rated 4 over a possible 2029 impact. A longer observed arc refined its orbit and dropped the rating to 0 by August 2006, and NASA JPL radar tracking on 2021-03-25 definitively ruled out any Apophis impact risk for at least the next 100 years. The near-Earth object 2023 DW was briefly rated Torino 1 in 2023 over a small chance of a 2046 impact before refined tracking removed it from the risk list. NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) Sentry system continuously re-assesses these ratings as new observations arrive, so a Torino number is a snapshot of current knowledge, not a fixed verdict.

For the single 2013 impact event that struck with no advance warning at all - so it was never Torino-rated beforehand - open the Chelyabinsk Meteor 3D Explorer. For the geometry of Apophis's one specific 2029 close approach, open the Apophis 2029 Flyby 3D Explorer.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached.

This is an educational approximation - the approach path, closest-approach distance, and on-screen asteroid size are exaggerated for visibility, not a to-scale orbital-dynamics or risk-assessment simulation. The five band colors and the three cited historical ratings are the real IAU/NASA-JPL-published values.
