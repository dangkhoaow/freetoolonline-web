The reading separates delivery copies from archive copies. A JPG already flagged at quality 50 should not be re-saved at 70, because the artefacts inside the file get baked in by every subsequent save; if 80 is the delivery target, the archive copy belongs above that, not at the same level. Reading the quality first prevents the silent quality-floor problem where every later save runs at or below an already-low starting point, and the original detail you can no longer recover.

The reading itself takes a single upload through our HTTPS processing service: the JPG header is parsed server-side to read the recorded quality marker, the value is returned to the page, and the upload copy is rotated out after the shared retention window. The tool reads the recorded quality marker only; it does not change or re-save the file. A check this small spares the second guess by reporting what the file already lost, so the next decision (keep, re-compress for delivery, or hand off to a separate compress tool) gets made on real numbers instead of "looks fine on this screen".

The accepted inputs are .jpeg and .jpg only: PNG, GIF, WebP, HEIC, and AVIF do not store a single quality scalar in the JPEG sense, so the upload filter and the server-side service both reject any non-JPEG extension before the read is attempted. The reason both layers reject the same set is that the quality marker the tool reads is a JPEG-specific header field, and the alternative formats either have no equivalent scalar (HEIC, AVIF, WebP) or use a lossless representation (PNG, GIF) where a "quality" answer would be a category error rather than a smaller number - a reader who lands here with a PNG is reading the right answer for the wrong file and should convert to JPG first. The page UX is single-file by design: one reading per upload rather than a folder iterator, so a batch of files is handled by re-uploading the next one rather than dropping a directory onto the input. Both constraints follow from the way the JPEG quality marker is recorded inside the file header - the format-specific reading and the per-upload UX are the same constraint applied at two layers.

Once the recorded quality number is back on the page, the next action branches on real numbers rather than a screen-side hunch: knowing whether the recorded quality is 50, 70, or 90 informs the next save in a way no visual check can match, and each band maps to both a meaning and a tool. A high band (around 85 and above) is the source for further work: keep it as the archive, re-save from it for new delivery targets, and take it into compress image to hit a smaller target with predictable loss. A mid band (roughly 65 to 85) has already been re-saved once and is best treated as a delivery copy: branch the next save from a separate higher-quality original stored elsewhere rather than from the JPG you just read, and do not feed it back through compress unless you can also produce that higher-band archive. A low band (below 65) is already a delivery copy at the bottom of its quality budget, where further re-saving only compounds the loss the read is meant to surface: stop re-compressing and go back to the original capture for a fresh master. In short, high-band readings are the source for further work while low-band readings are already delivery copies; the reading is a data input for that branching decision, not a transformation of the file.

When you have two .jpg files in front of you (the source and a delivered copy, say, or two versions of the same image you are choosing between), upload each separately and note the two quality numbers: the difference between the recorded markers is a direct before-and-after comparison that tells you how much quality the second save discarded, which is a more concrete answer than a screen-side visual check on a single device. Each file in the comparison is its own single HTTPS upload, so your archive and delivery copies stay on your own disk while the read happens against the transient copy; the data input you take away is the quality-marker number.

The single-file design also has a useful follow-up step. When you re-save a JPG through a separate compress tool and want to verify the result, read the output back here as a second upload: the second reading reports the quality marker recorded by the compressor, which makes the round-trip auditable in two numbers (input quality and output quality) rather than a screen-side feel. The shared upload pipeline still reports one reading per upload, so the verify step is its own deliberate second pass.
