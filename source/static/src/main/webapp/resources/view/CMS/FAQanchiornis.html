Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Anchiornis important?

It is one of the oldest known feathered dinosaurs, from about 160 million years ago, and a four-winged paravian - long feathers grew on both its arms and its legs. It is also one of the very few dinosaurs whose original color has been read directly from fossil evidence rather than guessed.

Do we actually know what color it was?

Yes, unusually. Li and colleagues (2010) studied the fossilized melanosomes preserved in its feathers and reconstructed a mostly grey-black body with a reddish-brown crown and white wing feathers tipped in black. Fine feather texture and small details are still an artistic reconstruction.

How big was Anchiornis?

The holotype specimen measures about 34 cm long and roughly 110 g. The largest known specimens reach around 60 to 62 cm long with a wingspan near 57 cm and a mass cited in the range of 0.6 to 1 kg. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to compare scale against a 1.8 m person.

What does "four-winged" mean?

Anchiornis grew long, pennaceous (vaned) feathers on both its arms and its legs, giving it four wing-like limbs rather than the two seen in modern birds. The same body plan appears in the younger, Early Cretaceous Microraptor.

What did it eat?

Anchiornis was a small carnivore, likely feeding on small animals such as insects and lizards, based on its size and tooth shape.

When and where was it found?

Fossils come from the Tiaojishan Formation in Liaoning Province, China, and the species was named Anchiornis huxleyi by Xu Xing and colleagues in 2009. The rock layer dates to the Late Jurassic, about 160 million years ago.
