Frequently Asked Questions

How many orbital shells does Starlink use?

SpaceX's first-generation Starlink constellation is authorized by the FCC to fly in five shells, each at its own altitude and inclination: 550 km/53.0 degrees, 540 km/53.2 degrees, 570 km/70.0 degrees, and two near-polar shells at 560 km/97.6 degrees.

Why does Starlink use several shells instead of one orbit?

Different inclinations cover different latitude bands and spread the satellite count across more orbital planes, which increases coverage density and gives more routing paths between satellites. The two near-polar shells (97.6 degrees) reach high-latitude regions the lower-inclination shells cannot.

How many satellites are in each shell?

Per the FCC-authorized design: Shell 1 and Shell 2 each carry 1,584 satellites, Shell 3 carries 720, Shell 4 carries 348, and Shell 5 carries 172 - a design total of 4,408 across all five shells.

Does this page show the current live satellite count?

No. It shows the FCC-authorized design capacity per shell. The actual number of working satellites changes daily as SpaceX launches replacements and retires older units, so a live count would be out of date the moment it was published.

Why are the shells drawn farther apart than they really are?

In reality all five shells sit within 540 to 570 km altitude - under 1 percent of Earth's radius apart, so drawn to true scale they would overlap into one indistinguishable band. This page exaggerates the radial spacing so each shell's ring and inclination are visible individually; the real altitude is always shown as text.

How is this different from the Satellite Orbit Classes explorer?

Satellite Orbit Classes teaches the generic LEO/MEO/GEO altitude bands that apply to any satellite. This page is specific to one real megaconstellation - Starlink's actual five shells with their real published altitudes, inclinations, and satellite counts.
