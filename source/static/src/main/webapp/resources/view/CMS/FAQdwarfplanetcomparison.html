Frequently Asked Questions

What does the Dwarf Planet Size Comparison 3D Explorer show?

All five IAU-recognized dwarf planets - Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris - as scale-accurate spheres lined up in real distance order from the Sun. Click a name to highlight one and read its published figures.

How is this different from Planet Size Comparison?

Planet Size Comparison lines up the Sun and the eight IAU planets. This page compares only the five officially recognized dwarf planets, which are much smaller and, except for Ceres, orbit far beyond Neptune.

Why is Ceres so much smaller and closer than the other four?

Ceres orbits the main Asteroid Belt between Mars and Jupiter at 2.77 AU, while Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris all orbit the Kuiper Belt or scattered disc beyond Neptune, 39 to 68 AU out. Ceres is also the smallest of the five at about 940 km across.

Is Haumea really not round?

Yes. Haumea spins once every roughly 3.9 hours - the fastest confirmed rotation for a body this large - which stretches it into a genuinely elongated shape. The scene renders it flattened for that reason, not as a rendering error.

Are there more than five dwarf planets?

The IAU currently recognizes exactly five. Several other bodies - Sedna, Quaoar, Gonggong, and Orcus among them - are large enough that many astronomers consider them likely dwarf planets too, but none has been formally classified yet.

Why did Pluto stop being called a planet?

In 2006 the IAU defined "planet" to require clearing its orbital neighborhood of other debris. Pluto shares the Kuiper Belt with many similar-sized objects, so it was reclassified as a dwarf planet rather than the ninth planet.

Is the spacing between the spheres to real scale?

No, and the panel says so. Sphere size follows real diameters, but the gaps are compressed for readability - Eris actually orbits roughly 25 times farther from the Sun than Ceres does, far more than the on-screen spacing shows.
