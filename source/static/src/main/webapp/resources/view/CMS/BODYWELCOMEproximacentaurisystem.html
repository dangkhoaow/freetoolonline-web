Proxima Centauri System 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-24

Proxima Centauri is the closest known star to the Sun, just 4.2465 light-years away - and it turned out to have planets of its own. Proxima b, announced in 2016 by a European team using the radial-velocity method, orbits inside the star's habitable zone every 11.2 days. A second, smaller world, Proxima d, was confirmed in 2022 on a tighter 5.12-day orbit, too close to the star to be habitable. A third, larger candidate, Proxima c, would take roughly 5.28 years to complete one orbit far out in the cold - it is still unconfirmed.

Proxima Centauri is a red dwarf: small, dim, and cool compared with the Sun, at only about one-eighth the Sun's mass. Being close enough to study in detail also revealed a complication - Proxima Centauri is an active flare star, and its own discovery team flagged stellar flares as an open question for whether any of its planets could hold onto an atmosphere at all. This scene's flare button lets you see what that radiation burst looks like reaching every planet in the system at once, right next to the habitable-zone band the flare is complicating.

  • Proxima d, b, and c, each on its own real relative orbit and period
  • A habitable-zone band toggle showing which world actually sits inside it
  • A flare button that plays a stellar flare radiating out to every planet
  • A per-planet table of real distances, orbital periods, and masses
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

Anyone who has heard "the closest star to the Sun has a habitable-zone planet" gets to see exactly where that planet sits, how close its two neighbors are, and why a flare-prone host star complicates the habitability story.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization, not an atmospheric or climate model. Orbit distances use a compressed, non-linear scale so the two close-in planets and the far outer candidate all fit in the same view - the table above has the real AU figures. The star and planets are enlarged for visibility, and orbital motion is heavily sped up.
