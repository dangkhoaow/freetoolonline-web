Frequently Asked Questions

How close will Apophis actually come to Earth?

On 13 April 2029, asteroid 99942 Apophis will pass about 31,600 km from Earth's surface - closer than the geostationary satellite ring, which orbits at about 35,786 km altitude.

Will Apophis hit Earth in 2029?

No. NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies has used decades of radar and optical tracking to rule out any impact risk from Apophis for at least the next 100 years.

Can I see Apophis with my own eyes during the flyby?

Yes, in favorable locations. During the close approach Apophis will reach about magnitude 3, making it naked-eye visible over parts of the Eastern Hemisphere (Africa, Europe, and western Asia) under dark skies.

How big is Apophis?

Radar observations put its diameter at roughly 340 to 450 m, with a refined estimate of about 375 m - roughly the length of several football fields, though far too small to see without a telescope or naked-eye viewing during the closest approach.

Is the asteroid's path on this page a real orbital simulation?

No. This page draws a simplified straight-line path that passes Earth at the real published minimum distance, not a numerically integrated hyperbolic trajectory. The date, minimum distance, diameter, and impact-risk clearance shown are the real published figures.

Where do these numbers come from?

NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) close-approach tables and the European Space Agency's Near-Earth Object Coordination Centre (NEOCC), which independently publish matching distance and date figures for the 2029 flyby.
