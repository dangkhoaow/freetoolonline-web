Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Dakotaraptor?

About 4.35 to 6 m long, often cited around 5.5 m - roughly the same size class as the already-shipped Utahraptor, though weight estimates of about 220 to 350 kg point to a lighter build. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the scale against a 1.8 m person.

Did Dakotaraptor really have feathers?

A row of quill knobs on its forearm bone (the ulna) is direct fossil evidence of wing feathers - unlike most raptors on this site, where feathering is only inferred from close relatives. The 3D model here still does not render feathers, since no feather impressions themselves were preserved, only the knobs that anchored them.

Where and when did Dakotaraptor live?

Its fossils come from the Hell Creek Formation in Harding County, South Dakota, USA, from rock less than 20 m below the boundary that marks the mass extinction 66 million years ago - making it one of the youngest dinosaur species known. It shared that world with Tyrannosaurus and Triceratops.

What did Dakotaraptor eat, and did it compete with Tyrannosaurus?

It was a carnivore. Living alongside adult tyrannosaurs, it likely avoided direct competition by hunting smaller or different prey rather than going after the same animals.

Where does the name Dakotaraptor come from?

The genus name combines "Dakota", for the state where it was found, with "raptor" ("seizer" or "thief" in Latin). Robert DePalma, David Burnham, Larry Martin, Peter Larson, and Robert Bakker named it in 2015; the species "steini" honors Walter "Bill" Stein of PaleoAdventures, who helped recover the fossils.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published fossil figures, but the skin color and texture are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. No skull fossil has been found for Dakotaraptor, so the head follows closely related raptors rather than this animal's own remains, and no feathers are rendered even though quill knobs are real evidence it had them. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values, with ranges cited because sources and estimation methods vary.
