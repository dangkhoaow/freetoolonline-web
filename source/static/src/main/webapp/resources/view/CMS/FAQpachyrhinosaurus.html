Frequently Asked Questions

How big was a real Pachyrhinosaurus?

The commonly cited range for the type species, P. canadensis, is about 6-8 m long and roughly 3-4 metric tons, though some sources give lower weight estimates around 1.8 t. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the scale against a person.

Why does it not have horns like Triceratops?

Pachyrhinosaurus is a close relative of Triceratops, but instead of long brow horns it evolved thick, flattened bony bosses over the nose and above the eyes, with small spikes along the frill margin in some individuals. Paleontologists think the boss served a similar display or combat role to other ceratopsian horns.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The frill, boss, and body proportions follow the fossil skeleton, but the skin color and texture are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values.

When and where did Pachyrhinosaurus live?

In the Late Cretaceous, roughly 73.5 to 71 million years ago, in what is now western North America. The genus was named in 1950 by Charles M. Sternberg from a skull collected in 1945-1946 in Alberta's Horseshoe Canyon Formation.

What was "Big Sam"?

Big Sam is the nickname for one of the largest known Pachyrhinosaurus skulls, belonging to the related species P. lakustai, uncovered during a 2024 excavation at the Pipestone Creek bonebed in Alberta. The skull measures about 138 cm long with a 43 cm nasal boss.
