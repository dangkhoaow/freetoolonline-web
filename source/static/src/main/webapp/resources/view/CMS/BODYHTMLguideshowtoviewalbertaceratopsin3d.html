How to View Albertaceratops in 3D

Last reviewed: 2026-07-18

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

Albertaceratops 3D Viewer runs a Late Cretaceous Alberta centrosaurine with unusually long brow horns - length about 5.8-7 m (Size vs human uses 6.5 m), mass about 2-3.5 tonnes. Drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person for scale, and click a body part for a fossil-sourced fact.


Open the Albertaceratops page and take the camera

Open the Albertaceratops 3D Viewer and wait for the facts panel to fill. When the status line says you can drag, orbit Albertaceratops with the pointer and zoom with the scroll wheel or a two-finger pinch. Fullscreen expands the wrapper so the controls stay visible beside the scene. After first paint a license-clean CC-BY-SA 4.0 glTF may swap in automatically from the CDN; if that load fails the page stays on the procedural ceratopsian with zero regression.


Use the four Albertaceratops controls under the canvas

Under the Albertaceratops canvas, four controls handle view and scale:

  • Fullscreen - fills the display while keeping the buttons visible
  • Stop rotation / Auto-rotate - freezes or resumes auto-spin
  • Idle motion - adds subtle breathing and leg sway; tap again for Stand still
  • Size vs human - shows a 1.8 m person at the honest length ratio used by the viewer (6.5 m representative within the published about 5.8-7 m range; Wikipedia about 5.8 m; NHM lists 7.0 m)

Read the Albertaceratops facts panel and click a body part

The Albertaceratops facts panel lists length about 5.8-7 m (Wikipedia about 5.8 m; NHM lists 7.0 m; Size vs human uses 6.5 m), hip height about 2-2.5 m for a large adult, mass about 2-3.5 tonnes commonly cited (Wikipedia about 3500 kg), the Late Cretaceous window about 80-75 million years ago in Alberta (often cited near 77.5 Mya, Oldman Formation), herbivore diet, and naming by Ryan in 2007 (type species A. nesmoi). Distinct from Triceratops (later chasmosaurine) and Pachyrhinosaurus (nasal boss). Long brow horns are unusual among centrosaurines. A short click - not a drag - on head, body, leg, or frill surfaces a short fossil fact. Soft-tissue color is an artistic reconstruction; when the CC-BY-SA 4.0 glTF by seth the yutyrannus via Printables loads, the status text notes a real model with that credit - a stylized reconstruction, not a laser scan. Body shape beyond the skull is inferred from related centrosaurines where postcrania are incomplete. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.
