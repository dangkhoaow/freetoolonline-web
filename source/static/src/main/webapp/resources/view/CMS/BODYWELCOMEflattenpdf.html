Picking the flatten engine is the first practical choice on this page: ImageMagick handles layered PDFs that benefit from a uniform raster pass, while GhostScript suits vector-heavy files where the line work must stay sharp through the bake. When ImageMagick is selected, the density picker - 72 DPI for the smallest archive, 150 by default for general sharing, 300 when the result must stay print-ready - lets the same upload land at the right file-size and quality trade-off for each downstream use without re-running the upload.


The value of flatten is permanence and parity, not just byte-count: it is the one PDF operation here that bakes form fields, annotations, and layered content into the page itself, so a form-filled tax document, a signed contract, or a layered scan stays visually identical no matter which reader the recipient opens it in, and the once-editable fields no longer carry the risk of accidental edits on the way through. For files heading into long-term archive storage the same pass strips interactive elements that some PDF/A validators reject, so one upload covers both sending-outward and archival needs. Compress a PDF online for free: how flatten relates

Flatten is not a dedicated compressor, but the layer-merge usually reduces file size on layered PDFs as a side effect - signed contracts, scanned forms, and design exports often shrink noticeably once the layers, form fields, and annotations bake into one rendered sheet. The primary gain remains permanence and parity (every reader opens the file identically), and the byte-count drop comes for free on inputs that have many layers to begin with. For a strictly compress-oriented workflow on flat documents, pick a tool whose job is image or document compression, since flatten will not shrink an already-single-layer PDF.
