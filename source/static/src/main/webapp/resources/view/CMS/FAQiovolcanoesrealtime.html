Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Io's surface yellow and orange?

Io's surface is covered in sulfur and sulfur dioxide compounds. The yellow and orange colors come from different elemental and chemical states of sulfur, which create vivid colorful patches across the moon. The explorer uses NASA color imaging to show Io's true appearance.

How hot do Io's volcanoes get?

Io's eruption temperatures range from 700 to 1700 Kelvin (427 to 1427 degrees Celsius). The hottest volcanoes like Pele are among the hottest known places in the Solar System. All temperatures displayed are from NASA Juno and Galileo spacecraft direct observations.

Why does Io have so many volcanoes compared to Earth?

Jupiter's enormous gravitational pull creates intense tidal heating as Io orbits the planet. This generates more internal heat than radioactive decay does on Earth. The result is continuous volcanic activity across Io's entire surface - over 500 confirmed active volcanoes, compared to Earth's ~600.

Is this explorer accurate or a game?

This is an educational visualization, not a physics simulation. All volcano locations come from NASA mission data (Voyager, Galileo, Juno, New Horizons). Temperature and height figures are NASA-published values. Sizes and distances on-screen are scaled for readability - see the reference table in the main guide for actual dimensions.

Can I use this explorer offline?

Yes. After loading once, the explorer caches the 3D scene locally. Return visits load instantly. If your browser loses connection, the cached version still works. No internet required after the first visit.

What's the difference between this and NASA's official Io maps?

This explorer is a simplified, interactive 3D view designed for learning. NASA's scientific maps contain thousands of named features and are research-grade. This explorer focuses on the ~10 most significant active volcanoes to keep the experience fast and understandable for a general audience.

How often are new volcano discoveries added?

Io's volcanoes are continuously monitored by NASA and ESA telescopes. The data in this explorer reflects observations through 2024 (Juno's latest flybys). Future discoveries will be added in periodic updates. Check back for new volcanoes as NASA analyzes new Juno images.
