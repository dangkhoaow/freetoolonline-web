Photon Sphere & ISCO 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-24

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

This browser explorer spins up a 3D black hole and shows exactly where light and matter can still orbit it, computed live from the real Kerr equations as you drag the spin slider.

Every non-spinning (Schwarzschild) black hole has two fixed boundaries around its event horizon: the photon sphere at 1.5 Schwarzschild radii, where light can loop in an unstable circular path, and the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) at 3 Schwarzschild radii, inside which no stable orbit exists for ordinary matter. Spin the black hole and both boundaries split into a prograde and a retrograde version, following the exact closed-form Kerr solution published by Bardeen, Press and Teukolsky in 1972: as the dimensionless spin parameter a* rises toward the astrophysical Thorne limit of about 0.998, the prograde photon orbit and ISCO pull in toward the horizon (down to 1 and 1.237 gravitational radii), while the retrograde versions push outward (up to 4 and 9 gravitational radii). The event horizon itself shrinks with spin, and a surrounding ergosphere - the region where frame-dragging forces everything, even light, to co-rotate - always touches the horizon at the poles and reaches exactly 2 gravitational radii at the equator, for any spin.

Kip Thorne showed in 1974 that ongoing accretion-disk torque limits a real black hole's spin to about a* = 0.998 - true extremal spin (a*=1) is never physically reached, which is why the slider stops just short of it. The 2019 Event Horizon Telescope image of M87* and the 2022 image of Sagittarius A* both show a bright ring consistent with the general-relativity-predicted lensed photon orbit, the strongest present observational support for the geometry this scene visualizes. Frame-dragging - the effect pulling the arrows around the ergosphere - is the same Lense-Thirring effect that NASA's Gravity Probe B mission measured directly around Earth between 2004 and 2011, to about 19% precision; near a spinning black hole the same physics is vastly amplified by strong gravity, not separately measured.

For the artistic, non-spinning accretion-disk view of a black hole, open the Black Hole 3D Explorer; for a general teaching grid of spacetime curvature, open the Spacetime Curvature 3D Explorer.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached.

This is an idealized Kerr-vacuum-solution explorer, not a numerical-relativity or ray-traced simulation - the four radii shown are exact analytic GR results at the spin you pick, but the ergosphere is drawn as a simplified oblate-spheroid approximation of its true shape, and the orbiting markers are teaching cues, not a photon or particle trajectory integrator.
