Vesta Rheasilvia Basin 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

Vesta is one of the largest bodies in the asteroid belt - mean diameter about 529 kilometers. Hubble saw a south-polar depression in 1997; NASA's Dawn spacecraft mapped it in 2011 and named the younger basin Rheasilvia after the mythological vestal Rhea Silvia. Dawn found Rheasilvia about 500 kilometers wide and roughly 19 kilometers deep, covering most of the southern hemisphere, with a central peak complex about 200 kilometers across that rises some 19 to 22 kilometers from the floor - one of the tallest mountains known in the Solar System.

Rheasilvia partly overlaps an older ~400 kilometer basin called Veneneia. Both basins are unexpectedly young, roughly 1 to 2 billion years, and their excavated debris is linked to the Vestoid asteroid family and HED meteorites. This explorer focuses on Rheasilvia's bowl and central peak so you can see how large the scar is relative to Vesta itself.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization - Vesta is a smooth ellipsoid, the basin and peak are schematic, and Veneneia is only a faint rim hint, not a full topographic mesh.
