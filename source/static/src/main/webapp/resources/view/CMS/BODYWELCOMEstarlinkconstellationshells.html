Starlink Constellation Shells 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

This browser explorer draws the five real orbital shells of SpaceX's Starlink megaconstellation, each colored and tilted to its own published altitude and inclination. Click a shell button to isolate it and read its real satellite count, or show all five together to see how the shells fit around Earth.

Starlink is not one orbit - it is a layered set of shells. Two of them share almost the same altitude and inclination (Shells 1 and 2, at 550 km/53.0 degrees and 540 km/53.2 degrees) to spread capacity across more orbital planes, while Shell 3 tilts further (70.0 degrees) and Shells 4 and 5 fly near-polar orbits (97.6 degrees) to cover high latitudes the lower-inclination shells miss.

  • Click any of the five shell buttons to isolate that shell and dim the rest
  • Read each shell's real altitude, inclination, and satellite count in the facts panel
  • Shell 1's orbital-plane structure (72 planes of 22 satellites) is shown when selected
  • Show all five shells together to see the full layered structure around Earth
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

These numbers come from SpaceX's FCC authorization for the Starlink Gen1 constellation (docket SAT-LOA-20170726-00110 and its amendments), as reported after the 2021 shell-completion milestone. The live number of satellites actually on orbit changes daily as SpaceX launches replacements and retires old units - this page shows the authorized design capacity per shell, not a live tracker.

This page focuses on the shell STRUCTURE - altitude, inclination, and satellite count per shell. For a single satellite's real-time ground track, see ISS Orbit Tracker 3D. For the generic altitude bands that classify any satellite as LEO, MEO, or GEO (not specific to Starlink), see Satellite Orbit Classes 3D Explorer.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached.

This is an educational approximation, not a live tracker: the radial spacing between shells is exaggerated here for visibility (the real altitudes are all within 540-570 km, less than 1 percent of Earth's radius apart), and each shell's dots are an illustrative sample rather than a 1-to-1 count of every satellite. Each shell's real altitude, inclination, and satellite count are shown as text and are accurate to the cited FCC filing.
