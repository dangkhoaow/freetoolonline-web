Placodus was a stocky marine reptile that lived in the Middle Triassic, roughly 247 to 237 million years ago, foraging on shellfish along the shallow coastal seas of what is now Central Europe and China. Published length estimates run about 2.5 to 3 m, with some sources citing closer to 2 m; it was not a dinosaur, and unlike streamlined open-ocean swimmers such as mosasaurs, it kept four sturdy legs for walking along the seafloor. Its name means "plate tooth": broad, flat teeth lined the roof of its mouth and lower jaw for crushing shellfish, while a few forward-pointing chisel-like teeth likely pried mollusks off rocks.

Placodus was named in 1833 by Louis Agassiz, and its fossils have been found across Central Europe - Germany, France, and Poland - as well as China. Thick, dense ribs (a condition called pachyostosis) added ballast to the body, much like a diver's weight belt, helping it stay submerged while it foraged.
