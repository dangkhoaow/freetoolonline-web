This page renders a Stegosaurus as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click the plated back, the spiked tail, or a leg to read a fact about that part.

Stegosaurus lived in the Late Jurassic, roughly 155 to 145 million years ago, with fossils found in the western United States and in Portugal. Because the genus includes more than one species and few complete skeletons exist, published length estimates vary: many individuals are cited at about 6.5 to 9 m, with the largest known individuals reaching close to 9 m, and weight estimates ranging from roughly 2.7 to 5.8 metric tons depending on the specimen. Two alternating rows of 17 to 22 bony plates ran from the neck to partway down the tail, with the largest plates - over the hips - exceeding 60 cm both wide and tall. The tail ended in four sharp spikes, each roughly 60 to 90 cm long, likely sheathed in keratin in life to make them longer and sharper than the bone alone suggests.

The genus was named in 1877 by American paleontologist Othniel Charles Marsh, based on a holotype (YPM 1850) collected that year near Morrison, Colorado, by fossil hunter Arthur Lakes. The name "Stegosaurus" means "roofed lizard" - Marsh originally, and incorrectly, believed the plates lay flat over the animal's back like roof tiles. Most researchers now agree the tail spikes were mainly a defensive weapon, while the back plates are thought to have served mainly for visual display, with a secondary and still-debated role in regulating body temperature. A notable specimen nicknamed "Sophie," acquired by London's Natural History Museum in 2014, is about 85% complete - one of the most complete stegosaur skeletons ever found, and the first to preserve every plate and spike in its original position.
