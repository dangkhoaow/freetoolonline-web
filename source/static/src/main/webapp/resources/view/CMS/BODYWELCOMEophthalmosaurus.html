This page renders Ophthalmosaurus as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a person beside it for scale, and click the head, a flipper, or the tail to read a fact about that part.

Ophthalmosaurus lived roughly 165 to 157 million years ago, in the Middle to Late Jurassic, and hunted fish and soft-bodied cephalopods with small, weakly developed teeth rather than the larger, heavier bite of some other ichthyosaurs. Its name means "eye lizard" - the bony sclerotic ring around each eye socket measures about 220 to 230 mm (roughly 9 in) across, proportionally the largest eyes of any known vertebrate, and likely helped it hunt in dim or deep water, possibly at night. A commonly cited scientific estimate (Motani et al.) puts a typical individual at about 4 m long and 930 to 950 kg, though some other sources give lengths up to around 6 m for the genus. It was not a dinosaur - it belonged to a separate group of marine reptiles - so the model shows a streamlined, dolphin-shaped body with a dorsal fin, paired paddle flippers, and a crescent-shaped tail fluke.

Ophthalmosaurus was described by British palaeontologist Harry Govier Seeley in 1874, based on fossils from the Oxford Clay Formation near Peterborough, England - a site that has produced many well-preserved ichthyosaur specimens from the Jurassic seas that once covered the region.
