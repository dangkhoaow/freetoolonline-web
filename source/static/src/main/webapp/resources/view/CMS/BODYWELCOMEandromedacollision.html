This explorer renders the real, published timeline for the Milky Way's eventual merger with the Andromeda Galaxy (M31) - scrub the Time slider from today out to about 10 billion years and watch two spiral disks close a 2.5-million-light-year gap, stretch into tidal tails, and settle into one merged galaxy.

The Spiral Galaxy 3D Explorer renders one galaxy's shape in isolation; this page renders what happens when two real galaxies - ours and our nearest large neighbor - actually meet. Andromeda is approaching at about 110 km/s, measured from its blueshift, and the classic Hubble-based scenario places the first close pass in roughly 3.75 to 4 billion years, with the two cores visible as a lobed pair by about 5.1 billion years and a full merger into one elliptical galaxy by about 7 billion years.

  • Two procedurally-rendered spiral disks (Milky Way and Andromeda) with a Time slider spanning 0-10 billion years
  • Approach, tidal-tail stretching, and merger phases driven by the real published timeline, with a facts panel that updates per phase
  • Play auto-advances the timeline; drag the slider to jump straight to any point in the merger
  • Honest probability caveat: a 2025 study briefly lowered the merger odds to about 50%, and a 2026 recalibration raised them back to about 90%
  • Runs in-browser with three.js - no account, no upload

Scrub past the 3.75-billion-year mark and the two disks visibly stretch into long tidal tails as gravity pulls stars out of their original orbits - the same distortion astronomers observe in real merging galaxy pairs today, such as the Antennae Galaxies. Keep scrubbing past 7 billion years and the tails wind back down into a single, roughly spherical galaxy with no obvious spiral structure left - the expected end state of a major galaxy merger.

This is an educational approximation tuned to teach the sequence, not a gravitational simulation - the disk shapes, tidal-tail stretching, and the speed of the on-screen timeline are illustrative, while the separation, approach speed, and Gyr milestones in the table above are the real published figures. Both galaxies are drawn far larger relative to their separation than their true scale so the merger stays visible on screen.

Renders on-device with WebGL - loads once (about 0.7 MB), cached by the browser, no data sent to a server.

The merger probability itself is a genuine example of science self-correcting as data improves: the 2025 estimate used the most precise Gaia proper-motion data available at the time and found real uncertainty from M33's and the Large Magellanic Cloud's orbits; the 2026 recalibration used improved Gaia data and pushed the estimate back toward the classic near-certain outcome. Neither study disputes the separation, approach speed, or merger timeline shown here - only the probability that it fully completes.
