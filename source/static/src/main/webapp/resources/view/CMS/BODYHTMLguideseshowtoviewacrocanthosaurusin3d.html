Acrocanthosaurus 3D Viewer runs an Early Cretaceous carcharodontosaurid theropod with a low dorsal ridge in your browser. Drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person for scale, and click head, neck, body, leg, tail, or arm for a fossil-sourced fact.


Open the Acrocanthosaurus page and take the camera

Open the Acrocanthosaurus 3D Viewer and wait for the facts panel to fill. Orbit with the pointer and zoom with the scroll wheel or a two-finger pinch. Fullscreen keeps the controls visible beside the scene. After first paint a license-clean glTF may swap in from the CDN; if that load fails the page stays on the procedural silhouette.


Use the four Acrocanthosaurus controls under the canvas

Under the Acrocanthosaurus canvas, four controls handle view and scale:

  • Fullscreen - fills the display while keeping the buttons visible
  • Stop rotation / Auto-rotate - freezes or resumes auto-spin
  • Idle motion - adds subtle body sway; tap again for Stand still
  • Size vs human - shows a 1.8 m person at the honest panel length ratio (11.5 m)

Read the Acrocanthosaurus facts panel and click a body part

The Acrocanthosaurus facts panel lists length about 11-11.5 m (panel baseline 11.5 m), weight about 4.4-8.4 t (panel baseline 6.2 t), height about 3.5 m, era 125-100 Mya Early Cretaceous Antlers and Twin Mountains formations south-central North America, carnivore diet, named by J. Willis Stovall and Wann Langston in 1950 with best-known specimen NCSM 14345 Fran from Oklahoma. Tall neural spines interpreted as supporting neck/back musculature and a low muscular dorsal ridge rather than a tall Spinosaurus-style sail. Early Cretaceous North American apex predator. Soft-tissue color is an artistic reconstruction. Optional CC0/PD glTF (Model Magic via Printables 1768652) may swap in. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.


What the Acrocanthosaurus how-to guide is not
