Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Torino Impact Hazard Scale?

A 0-10 integer scale developed by Richard Binzel (MIT), adopted by the IAU in 1999 and revised in 2005, that combines an asteroid's impact probability and expected consequence into one public-facing number.

What do the five color bands mean?

0 White is no hazard, 1 Green is a normal event with no unusual danger, 2-4 Yellow merits attention by astronomers, 5-7 Orange is a threatening event, and 8-10 Red is a certain collision.

What was Apophis's Torino rating in 2004?

4 - the highest rating ever assigned to a real object at the time, given on 2004-12-27 over a possible 2029 impact.

Why did Apophis's rating drop to 0?

A longer observed arc through August 2006 refined its orbit enough to remove the impact risk. NASA JPL radar tracking on 2021-03-25 later definitively ruled out any Apophis impact for at least the next 100 years.

What happened with 2023 DW?

It was briefly rated Torino 1 in 2023 over a small chance of a 2046 impact. Refined tracking later removed it from the risk list.

Who keeps these ratings up to date?

NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) Sentry system continuously re-assesses Torino and Palermo ratings as new observations arrive - a rating is a snapshot of current knowledge, not a fixed verdict.

How is this different from the Chelyabinsk and Apophis-2029-flyby pages?

The Chelyabinsk meteor struck in 2013 with no advance warning, so it was never Torino-rated beforehand. The Apophis-2029-flyby page covers the geometry of one specific 2029 close approach. This page is the classification scale itself, compared across several real historical cases.

Is the on-screen approach path real physics?

The band colors and the three cited ratings are the real IAU/NASA-JPL-published values. The approach path, closest-approach distance, and on-screen asteroid size are exaggerated for visibility, not a to-scale orbital-dynamics simulation.
