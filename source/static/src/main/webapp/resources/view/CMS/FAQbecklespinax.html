Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Becklespinax?

Because only three back vertebrae survive, size estimates are indirect. Gregory Paul (1988) estimated about 7.6 m long and about 1 tonne; other published estimates run up to about 9 m and 1.8 tonnes. The facts panel uses the 7.6 m and 1 t figures as mid-range values. Turn on the human figure to compare against a 1.8 m person.

What fossils of Becklespinax actually exist?

The entire known fossil record of Becklespinax is three fused posterior dorsal vertebrae with unusually tall neural spines, found in the Wadhurst Clay Formation near Battle, East Sussex, England, and held at the Natural History Museum, London (specimen NHMUK R1828). No skull, limb, or tail bones are known.

Why has this dinosaur had different names?

Richard Owen first described the vertebrae in 1856. Over the following century they were referred to several names, including Altispinax dunkeri, before George Olshevsky placed them in a new genus, Becklespinax, in 1991, naming it after collector Samuel Husband Beckles.

Did it have a sail like Spinosaurus?

Probably not a tall sail. The unusually high neural spines are generally interpreted as supporting a low muscular ridge along the back, similar to the pattern seen in Acrocanthosaurus, rather than a tall Spinosaurus-style sail.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The published figures are real, but because only three vertebrae are known, the body shape, arms, legs, head, and skin color shown here are an artistic reconstruction, not a fossil-accurate skeleton.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
