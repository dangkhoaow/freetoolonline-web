This page renders a Beipiaosaurus as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click the head, an arm, or the tail to read a fact about that part.

Beipiaosaurus lived in the Early Cretaceous, roughly 125 million years ago, in what is now the Yixian Formation of Liaoning Province, China - the same fossil-rich beds that preserved many other feathered dinosaurs. Published length estimates run about 1.8 to 2.6 m, and it is one of the smallest and most basal known members of the therizinosaur family, a group of theropods that mostly grew into large, slow-moving herbivores such as Therizinosaurus. What makes Beipiaosaurus scientifically important is that it is the species that gave the first direct fossil evidence of feathers anywhere in the therizinosaur family: long, simple, unbranched filamentous proto-feathers were preserved alongside the skeleton, so feathering on the larger and later Therizinosaurus is inferred from this smaller relative rather than observed directly.

Beipiaosaurus was named by Xu, Tang and Wang in 1999, from a holotype (specimen IVPP V11559) found in the Yixian Formation. Published mass estimates vary widely by method, from about 27 kg in one study up to roughly 85-135 kg in others; this page uses 85 kg as a commonly cited mid-range figure. Unlike the huge, sickle-like claws of later therizinosaurs, its forelimbs were elongated but modestly clawed, a trait it retained from earlier, non-herbivorous theropod ancestors.
