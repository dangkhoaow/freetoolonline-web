When to Play a Retro FPS in Your Browser

Last reviewed: 2026-07-07

Retro FPS Online runs a complete classic-style first-person shooter - the freely licensed game Freedoom - inside a browser tab. Nothing is installed and no account is created, which makes it the right pick for some situations and the wrong one for others. This page covers which is which.


When it fits

It shines when you want the classic run-and-gun feel without touching the system: a quick session on a computer where you cannot (or do not want to) install games, revisiting the 1990s style legally - Freedoom is a complete free game, not a demo or a pirated copy - or having a shooter that keeps working offline once its one-time download is cached in the browser. Saves stay in the browser between visits, and an export file moves them to another computer.


When it does not fit

This is a keyboard-first game: arrows move, Ctrl fires, Space opens doors. A phone or tablet without a keyboard is the wrong device for it, and the page says so instead of promising touch controls. There is also no multiplayer, no accounts and no leaderboards - the game runs entirely in the tab. And if you want a heavily modded setup or a commercial campaign, a desktop engine install is the right tool; this page deliberately ships only freely licensed content - see how it compares to the alternatives.


What to expect on first use

Each campaign card states its one-time download before anything is fetched - about 11 MB per campaign - and later visits start from local storage. Campaign 1 is four episodes (36 levels); Campaign 2 is 32 levels in one continuous run. The engine renders widescreen at your display's frame rate and synthesizes its music on-device, so the classics feel smooth rather than shrunken. One campaign runs per page load; switching reloads the page and saves are unaffected.
