Frequently Asked Questions

How big was a real Dunkleosteus?

That is genuinely disputed. Older, widely repeated sources cite lengths up to about 8-10 m and weights near 4 tonnes, but a 2023 statistical study suggested most individuals were closer to 3.4-4.1 m, since only the head and chest armor commonly fossilize. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the scale against a person.

Was Dunkleosteus a dinosaur?

No. Dunkleosteus was an armored fish - a placoderm - that lived during the Late Devonian period, tens of millions of years before the first dinosaurs appeared. It swam in the sea rather than walking on land.

Did Dunkleosteus have teeth?

Not true teeth. Its jaw was made of sharp bony plates that formed self-sharpening, blade-like edges, strong enough to crush the armor of other fish. A 2006 biomechanical study estimated its bite force at around 7,400 newtons at the jaw tip, similar to Tyrannosaurus rex and modern crocodiles.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The body proportions follow the published fossil record, and the length shown uses the smaller, more recent 2023 estimate rather than the older, still-debated figure. Even so, skin texture and color are not preserved for an animal this old, so the surface shown here is an artistic reconstruction; the length, era, and bite-force figures are real published values.

When and where did it live?

Roughly 382 to 358 million years ago, during the Late Devonian period. Its fossils have been found across North America, with the first specimens discovered in 1867 near Sheffield Lake, Ohio, on the shore of Lake Erie.

Do I need to install anything to view it?
