Frequently Asked Questions

What is a coronal mass ejection?

A CME is a large expulsion of plasma and magnetic field from the Sun's corona. Speeds range from slower than 250 km/s to nearly 3000 km/s (NOAA SWPC), and masses can reach billions of tons (~1e16 g class, NASA GSFC).

How long does a CME take to reach Earth?

The fastest Earth-directed CMEs can arrive in 15-18 hours. Slower and typical events take several days; common arrivals are often about 1-3 days (NOAA SWPC).

How is this different from the Carrington Event page?

This explorer teaches the general CME speed range and Sun-to-Earth travel clock. The Carrington Event page focuses on one historical storm in 1859 - open that sibling when you want that specific event.

How is this different from the solar-wind heliosphere page?

Solar wind is the ambient outflow that fills the heliosphere. A CME is a discrete ejection riding through that medium. Use the Solar Wind Heliosphere explorer for the ambient bubble; use this page for a discrete cloud and arrival estimate.

Are the on-screen distances to scale?

No. The Sun-Earth gap is compressed so the expanding cloud stays readable. Travel time uses the real 1 AU distance of 149,600,000 km divided by your scrubbed speed.

Does this page solve MHD solar-wind equations?

No. It is an educational explorer using published NOAA SWPC and NASA GSFC figures. It does not solve magnetohydrodynamics or predict real-time space weather.
