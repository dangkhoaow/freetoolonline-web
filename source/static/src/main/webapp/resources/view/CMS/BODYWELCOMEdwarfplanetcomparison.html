Dwarf Planet Size Comparison 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

This explorer lines up the five IAU-recognized dwarf planets - Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris - as scale-accurate spheres in real distance order, so the size difference a chart of numbers hides becomes visible at a glance: Eris and Pluto are close in size to each other, while Ceres is barely a fifth as wide as either.

Clicking a name highlights that world, jumps the camera to it, and opens its row in the comparison table below. This page compares the five dwarf planets to each other - for the eight IAU planets and the Sun on the same kind of scale-accurate lineup see Planet Size Comparison 3D Explorer, and for the Kuiper Belt region several of these bodies orbit within, shown as a structure rather than individual worlds, see Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud 3D Explorer.

  • All five IAU-recognized dwarf planets rendered at once - Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, Eris
  • Sphere size is proportional to each body's published diameter
  • Left-to-right order follows real distance from the Sun, not just size
  • Haumea renders slightly flattened because its fast ~3.9-hour rotation genuinely stretches it into an elongated shape
  • Comparison table lists diameter, distance, orbital period, and region for each body
  • Runs in-browser with three.js - no account, no upload

Ceres stands apart from the other four in more than distance: it is the only dwarf planet in the inner solar system, orbiting the main Asteroid Belt between Mars and Jupiter, while Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris all orbit far beyond Neptune. Ceres was also the first of the five to be found, in 1801, more than two centuries before the 2006 vote that created the "dwarf planet" category in the first place.

This is an educational size comparison, not an orbital simulation - the row spacing between spheres is compressed for readability and is not linear with real distance (Eris orbits roughly 25 times farther from the Sun than Ceres does). Haumea's flattened shape is a genuine physical feature, not a rendering error: its rapid spin - the fastest confirmed for any body this large - pulls its own surface outward at the equator.

Renders on-device with WebGL - loads once (about 0.7 MB), cached by the browser, no data sent to a server.

The IAU currently recognizes exactly five dwarf planets. Several other bodies, including Sedna, Quaoar, Gonggong, and Orcus, are large enough that many astronomers consider them likely dwarf planets too, but they have not yet been formally classified - a reminder that the boundary between "dwarf planet" and "large Kuiper Belt object" is still an active area of debate, not a settled list.
