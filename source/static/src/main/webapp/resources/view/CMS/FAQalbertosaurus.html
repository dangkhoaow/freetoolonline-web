Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Albertosaurus?

Typical adults are cited around 9 m long and about 3.4 m tall at the hip, weighing roughly 1.8-2.7 t (2-3 short tons). The largest known individual, recovered from the Dry Island Bonebed, is estimated to have exceeded 10 m. This page uses 9 m and 2.2 t as facts-panel baselines. Turn on the human figure to compare with a 1.8 m person.

What kind of dinosaur was Albertosaurus?

It was a tyrannosaurid theropod, a smaller and more lightly built relative of Tyrannosaurus rex. It belongs to the albertosaurine branch of the tyrannosaurid family, alongside the closely related Gorgosaurus.

When and where did Albertosaurus live?

In Late Cretaceous rocks of Alberta, Canada, roughly 76-70 million years ago. A partial skull was found in 1884 by Joseph Burr Tyrrell; Henry Fairfield Osborn named the species Albertosaurus sarcophagus in 1905.

What is the Dry Island Bonebed?

A fossil site in Alberta, discovered by Barnum Brown, containing the remains of at least 26 Albertosaurus individuals - the largest known concentration of a large Cretaceous theropod at a single site. It is one reason some researchers think tyrannosaurids may have been at least loosely gregarious, though the fossils cannot fully settle how the group came together.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published figures, but the skin color and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values, with ranges cited where sources disagree.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
