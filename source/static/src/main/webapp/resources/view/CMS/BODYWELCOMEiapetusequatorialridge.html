Iapetus Equatorial Ridge 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

Iapetus is Saturn's third-largest moon - about 1,400 kilometers across in NASA summaries, with more precise mean diameters near 1,470 kilometers in modern shape models. Cassini first clearly imaged a seam of mountains almost exactly on the equator on 31 December 2004. That equatorial ridge runs roughly 1,300 kilometers, with peaks rising up to about 20 kilometers above the plains - among the tallest mountain structures in the Solar System for such a small body - and gives Iapetus its famous walnut silhouette.

The moon is also famously two-toned: a dark leading hemisphere (Cassini Regio) and a bright icy trailing side. Ridge origin is still debated - ideas range from internal tectonics to accretion of an ancient ring onto the equator - so this page shows the ridge geometry and albedo contrast without picking a single formation story.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization - the ridge height is exaggerated for readability, the body is a smooth sphere plus torus seam, and albedo is a simple half-and-half sketch, not a Cassini mosaic.
