Europa Subsurface Ocean 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

Peel back Jupiter's moon Europa layer by layer to see the ice shell, the ocean underneath, the rocky mantle, and the metallic core.

Drag to orbit the view, scroll or pinch to zoom, and slide Peel shell from 0 to 100 percent to cut through each layer in order - ice shell first, then ocean, then mantle, then core. Click any exposed layer for its facts, or jump straight to a layer with the four quick buttons.

The facts panel lists the real published depth ranges: an ice shell about 15-25 km thick over a liquid-water ocean about 60-150 km deep, an ocean believed to hold roughly twice the water of all of Earth's oceans combined.

  • Peel shell slider cuts a wedge through the ice, ocean, mantle, and core, layer by layer
  • Ice shell, Ocean, Mantle, and Core quick buttons jump straight to a layer
  • Clipper flyby tracks toggle shows six illustrative ground-track arcs on the ice shell
  • Real published figures in the panel; on-screen layer thickness is exaggerated and disclosed
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

Students use the peel slider to see why an icy moon can hide a global ocean, and readers curious about NASA's Europa Clipper mission click the flyby-tracks toggle to see how a spacecraft samples a world it cannot land on.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

The scene is an educational visualization tuned to teach the interior layer order - it does not compute a geophysical model of Europa's interior. Layer thicknesses on screen are exaggerated so the ice shell and ocean stay visible; at true scale they are under 2 percent of Europa's radius.

For a step-by-step walkthrough, read the Europa Subsurface Ocean 3D Explorer step-by-step guide, or see when to use it. The Space 3D collection also includes a Galilean Moons Explorer and an Enceladus Geysers Explorer for other icy moons.
