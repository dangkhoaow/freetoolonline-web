Circumbinary Planet 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-18

In September 2011, NASA's Kepler space telescope confirmed something science fiction had imagined but nature had never definitively shown: a planet orbiting two stars at once. The planet, Kepler-16b, quickly earned the nickname "Tatooine" after Luke Skywalker's twin-sunned home world in Star Wars, though the real planet is a cold gas giant rather than a desert world. It lies roughly 200 to 245 light-years away, and its discovery was unusually precise - the planet's radius is known to within about 0.3 percent, tighter than most planets beyond our solar system.

The Kepler-16 system has three moving parts, all orbiting nearly in the same flat plane. Two stars - a K-type dwarf about 69 percent the Sun's mass and a much smaller red dwarf about 20 percent the Sun's mass - circle their shared center of mass every 41 days on a noticeably eccentric orbit, about 0.23 AU apart on average. Farther out, the planet itself, about three-quarters Jupiter's radius and a third of its mass, similar in size to Saturn, completes a much wider, nearly circular orbit around that same center every 228.8 days, roughly the distance Venus keeps from the Sun. Kepler found the system by catching the planet crossing in front of both stars in turn, on top of the two stars already eclipsing each other - an unusually rich set of overlapping signals that let astronomers pin down every mass and size with rare precision.

  • Two stars, a K dwarf and a smaller red dwarf, orbiting their shared center every 41 days
  • A Saturn-sized planet circling both stars every 228.8 days

Science-fiction fans get to compare the real system to the movie; students see how three-body orbital mechanics can still be stable and nearly flat; anyone who has heard the word "Tatooine" in the news can see the actual planet behind the headline.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization, not a real ephemeris - star and planet sizes are enlarged relative to their orbits for visibility, and the motion is heavily sped up; the real system is not to scale here, and Kepler-16b is a gas giant with no solid surface, unlike its fictional desert-world namesake.
