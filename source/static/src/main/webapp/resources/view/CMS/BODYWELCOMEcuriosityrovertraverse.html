Curiosity Rover Traverse 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

This browser explorer replays NASA's Curiosity rover moving through Gale Crater, one real mission milestone at a time, from its 2012 touchdown to its most recently reported position on Mount Sharp.

Curiosity landed at Bradbury Landing inside Gale Crater, a crater about 155 km across, on 2012-08-06. By Sol 669 (2014-06-24) it had completed one full Mars year - 687 Earth days - of surface operations. By Sol 753 (2014-09-19) it had driven nearly 9 km to reach Pahrump Hills, the base of Mount Sharp (Aeolis Mons), a mountain that rises about 5.5 km above the crater floor. On Sol 1,750 (2017-07-09) its onboard spectrometers confirmed hematite, an iron-oxide mineral first spotted from orbit, on a ridge later informally named Vera Rubin Ridge; the rover then spent Sol 1,800 to Sol 2,300 (Sept 2017 - Jan 2019) climbing that roughly 6.5 km ridge. As of the most recently reported update (Nov 2025), Curiosity is on Mount Sharp's lower slopes near Gediz Vallis Ridge, in "boxwork"-textured terrain around 4.6°S, 137.4°E, having driven a total of about 36.1 km since landing. These figures come from NASA JPL's Mars Science Laboratory mission pages and NASA Science's published mission maps and progress reports.

  • Milestone slider (6 stops) moves the rover along a procedural route through Gale Crater toward Mount Sharp
  • Each stop shows its real sol number (where published), date, name, and distance driven since landing (where officially reported)
  • A running table lists all 6 milestones at once, with the active one highlighted
  • The rover's beacon and the route color mark ground already covered versus milestones still ahead
  • Drag to orbit, scroll or pinch to zoom
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

For a static, sitewide relief map built from real NASA MOLA elevation data covering all of Mars rather than one rover's path inside one crater, open the Mars Terrain 3D Explorer.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached.

This is an educational approximation - the crater, mountain, and route are procedural shapes, not a terrain-accurate reconstruction of Gale Crater, and the scene does not interpolate a continuous position between milestones or claim a live rover feed. Each milestone's sol number, date, and description are real and independently published by NASA JPL and NASA Science.
