Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Mamenchisaurus?

For the most fully documented mid-size species M. hochuanensis, published figures put length at about 18 m and mass at about 11.8 tonnes, with a neck longer than 9 m - roughly half of total body length. A separate species, M. sinocanadorum, is often cited for a neck about 15 m long, but that claim rests on only a few bones. Turn on the human figure to compare with a 1.8 m person.

Did Mamenchisaurus have the longest neck of any animal?

The longest-neck claim usually refers to M. sinocanadorum and is based on a handful of vertebrae plus one rib found in 1987. CT scans of those vertebrae found roughly 69-77 percent of the bone volume was air. This page does not blend that fragmentary record into the M. hochuanensis baseline body shown in the facts panel.

When and where did Mamenchisaurus live?

Most species lived in the Late Jurassic of China, about 160-145 million years ago, in the Sichuan Basin and Yunnan (formations such as the Upper Shaximiao and Shishugou). The genus was named by C.C. Young (Yang Zhongjian) in 1954; the type species is M. constructus.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published size figures for M. hochuanensis, but the skin color and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton. The length, neck, weight, and age figures shown are real published values, with the sinocanadorum record kept as a separate honesty note.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
