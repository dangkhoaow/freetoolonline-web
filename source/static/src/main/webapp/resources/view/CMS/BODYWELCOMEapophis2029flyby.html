Apophis 2029 Flyby 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

Reviewed by FreetoolOnline editorial team

This browser explorer draws asteroid 99942 Apophis's real close approach to Earth on 13 April 2029. Drag the slider to move through the flyby and watch the asteroid thread just inside Earth's own ring of geostationary satellites, with the live distance shown in the facts panel.

Apophis is a real, tracked near-Earth asteroid, not a hypothetical scenario. On 13 April 2029 it passes about 31,600 km from Earth's surface - closer than the satellites that broadcast television and relay weather data from geostationary orbit at about 35,786 km altitude. NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies has used decades of radar and optical tracking to rule out any impact risk for at least the next 100 years.

  • Watch the live distance readout cross inside the geostationary ring at closest approach

These figures come from NASA JPL's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) close-approach tables and the European Space Agency's Near-Earth Object Coordination Centre (NEOCC), which independently publish the same distance and date. Both agree Apophis will pass inside geostationary altitude - a genuinely close approach by asteroid standards, without any impact risk.

This page focuses on the 2029 flyby geometry itself - how close Apophis comes and when. For what happens when a much smaller object actually does reach the ground rather than passing by, see Chelyabinsk Meteor 3D Explorer. For how an impact crater forms on contact, see Impact Crater Formation 3D Explorer.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached.

This is an educational approximation, not an orbital simulation: the flyby path drawn here is a simplified straight line that passes Earth at the real published minimum distance, not a numerically integrated hyperbolic trajectory. Apophis itself is drawn far larger than true scale (a real object about 375 m across would be invisible at this zoom) so it can be seen and clicked. The date, minimum distance, diameter, and impact-risk clearance are the real published figures.
