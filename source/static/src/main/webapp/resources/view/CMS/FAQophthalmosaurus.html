Frequently Asked Questions

Was Ophthalmosaurus a dinosaur?

No. Ophthalmosaurus was a marine reptile - a member of the Ichthyosauria - that lived alongside dinosaurs but is a separate group. This viewer includes it because it is one of the most-searched prehistoric marine animals.

Why were its eyes so big?

Ophthalmosaurus had the proportionally largest eyes of any known vertebrate - the bony sclerotic ring around each eye socket measures about 220-230 mm (roughly 9 in) across. Researchers think this helped it see and hunt in dim or deep water, possibly at night.

How big was Ophthalmosaurus?

A commonly cited scientific estimate puts a typical individual at about 4 m long and 930-950 kg, though some other sources give lengths up to around 6 m for the genus. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to judge the scale.

What did Ophthalmosaurus eat?

Fish and soft-bodied cephalopods (squid-like animals). Its teeth were small and only weakly developed - suited to grabbing soft prey rather than crushing hard shells.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The body shape - a streamlined, dolphin-like torso, a dorsal fin, paired paddle flippers, and a crescent tail fluke - follows the fossil skeleton, but the skin color and texture are an artistic reconstruction. Fossils preserve bone, not color. The figures shown are real published values.

Where was Ophthalmosaurus first found?

Near Peterborough, England, in the Oxford Clay Formation. It was described by British palaeontologist Harry Govier Seeley in 1874.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached.
