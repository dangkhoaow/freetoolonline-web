This page renders a Dakotaraptor as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click the head, tail, an arm, or a leg to read a fact about that part.

Dakotaraptor lived in the Late Cretaceous, roughly 68 to 66 million years ago, and its fossils come from the Hell Creek Formation in Harding County, South Dakota, USA - found less than 20 m below the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary, making it one of the youngest dinosaur species known before the mass extinction. Published figures put an adult at about 4.35 to 6 m long, often cited around 5.5 m, putting it in the same size class as the already-shipped Utahraptor - though weight estimates of about 220 to 350 kg suggest a lighter build for its length. A sickle-shaped foot claw reaching about 24 cm along its outer curve tipped the second toe of each foot, among the largest recorded for any dromaeosaurid ("raptor"). A row of quill knobs on the forearm bone (ulna) is direct fossil evidence of wing feathers - unlike most raptors on this site, where feathering is only inferred from close relatives. As a carnivore sharing its Hell Creek habitat with Tyrannosaurus and Triceratops, it likely filled an ecological niche between small predators and the giant tyrannosaurs, possibly avoiding direct competition by hunting different prey or using a different strategy.

Robert DePalma, David Burnham, Larry Martin, Peter Larson, and Robert Bakker named the genus in 2015; the species "steini" honors Walter "Bill" Stein of PaleoAdventures, who helped recover the fossils. No skull material has been found for Dakotaraptor, so its head shape is reconstructed from closely related raptors rather than direct fossil evidence. A handful of bones in the original type material that were first identified as wishbones (furculae) were later reidentified by other researchers as fragments of turtle shell, a reminder that sorting out which bones belong to which animal at a bonebed site like this can take more than one study.

Everything runs on your device with WebGL, so the model works without an account and without sending anything to a server. The skin tone and pattern are an artistic reconstruction, because fossils do not preserve color or soft tissue, and this model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton; no feathers are rendered here even though quill knobs are real evidence Dakotaraptor had them, and no skull fossil is known so the head follows related raptors rather than this animal's own remains.
