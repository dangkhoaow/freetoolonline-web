Frequently Asked Questions

What does "Maiasaura" mean?

Maiasaura means "good mother lizard" in Latinized Greek. The name reflects the discovery of fossils showing that Maiasaura cared for their young at the nest - behavior that was rare or unknown in reptiles when fossils were first interpreted in 1979.

How do we know Maiasaura nested in herds?

The Egg Mountain fossil site in Montana preserved thousands of eggs arranged in nesting colonies, juvenile skeletons of various growth stages, embryos inside unhatched eggs, and adult bone beds. This evidence suggested parental care and herd nesting behavior - similar to modern birds and crocodilians.

Was Maiasaura a meat-eater or plant-eater?

Maiasaura was a herbivore. It had a specialized duck-like bill (lambeosaur features) designed for stripping and chewing leaves, plants, and cycads. Hadrosaurs often fed in herds on low-growing vegetation.

How big was a young Maiasaura?

Fossil juvenile Maiasaura specimens range from 1 to 6 meters long. Hatchlings were probably small (estimates 0.5 - 1 meter), and growth continued until they reached the adult 9 - 10 meter length. Nesting colonies show multiple juvenile sizes, suggesting the young stayed at the nest until they reached a few meters long.

When did Maiasaura live?

Maiasaura lived 75 million years ago during the Late Cretaceous (Campanian stage), in what is now Montana, USA. This was about 10 million years before the mass extinction that killed the non-avian dinosaurs.

Is this model scientifically accurate?

The skeleton, proportions, and posture are based on real fossil anatomy and modern paleontological research. However, skin color, exact muscle structure, and surface texture are not directly known from fossils and represent an artistic interpretation based on comparative anatomy (modern reptiles and birds) and fossil trace evidence. The model honors what we know while being honest about what remains uncertain.
