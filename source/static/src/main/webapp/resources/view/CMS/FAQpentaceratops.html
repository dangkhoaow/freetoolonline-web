Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Pentaceratops called "five-horned face"?

The name refers to two brow horns, one nose horn, and two smaller pointed cheek projections beneath the frill - five horn-like structures on the skull in total.

How big was Pentaceratops?

Published estimates usually fall around 5.5 to 7 m long (commonly cited near 6 m) and about 2.5 to 3 tonnes. Its skull alone ran about 2.16 to 2.3 m in known specimens. Turn on the human figure to see the scale against a 1.8 m person.

When and where did it live?

In the Late Cretaceous, about 76 to 73 million years ago, in the San Juan Basin of New Mexico (Kirtland and Fruitland Formations). The holotype skull was collected there in the early 1920s.

How is it different from Triceratops or Albertaceratops?

Triceratops has three facial horns and a shorter frill. Albertaceratops is an earlier centrosaurine from Alberta with long brow horns but no cheek projections. Pentaceratops is a chasmosaurine with an especially large frill and the extra pair of cheek projections that give it its name. This site has separate viewers for comparison.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published figures, but soft-tissue color is an artistic reconstruction. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton. Length, weight, and age figures are real published values, with ranges cited because sources vary.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
