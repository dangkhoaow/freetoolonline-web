Shepherd Moons 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-18

Planetary rings ought to be untidy. Because particles closer to a planet orbit faster than those farther out, any narrow band of debris should smear itself into a broad, fuzzy sheet within a few orbits. Yet some rings are startlingly thin and sharp. The explanation, worked out in 1979 by Peter Goldreich and Scott Tremaine, is the shepherd moon: a small satellite orbiting just inside or just outside a ring, whose gravity keeps the particles from wandering. This explorer draws a ring with a shepherd on each side and lets you switch their herding on and off.

The trick is a give-and-take of orbital energy. A particle that drifts inward speeds up and catches the inner moon, whose gravity tugs it forward and lifts it back out; a particle that drifts outward slows down and lets the outer moon catch up, and that pull drags it back in. Between the two moons the herd is penned into a crisp ribbon. Voyager 1 found Prometheus and Pandora straddling Saturn thin F ring in 1980, and Voyager 2 found Cordelia and Ophelia guarding Uranus epsilon ring in 1986. The Uranus pair is the clean textbook case; the F ring turned out to be far more chaotic, dominated by Prometheus alone, which also kicks up the ring famous kinks and streamers.

  • A ringed planet with a narrow band of particles and a shepherd moon on each side
  • A toggle that turns the shepherding on and off
  • With shepherds on, a thin crisp ringlet held between the two moons
  • With shepherds off, the band shearing and spreading into a broad disc
  • A speed slider and the real Prometheus, Pandora, Cordelia and Ophelia figures
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

Students see why thin rings need help to stay thin; teachers link the herding to Voyager discoveries; the curious watch a ring fall apart and snap back at the flick of a switch.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization - the confinement is illustrative rather than a gravity solver, sizes and distances are not to scale, and the motion is sped up.
