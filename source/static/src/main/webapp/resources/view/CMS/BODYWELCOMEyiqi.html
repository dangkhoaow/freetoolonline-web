Yi qi 3D Viewer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-24

This page renders Yi qi as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click the head, body, a wing, or a leg to read a fact about that part.

Yi qi is a small dinosaur described from a single fossil found in Hebei, China, and it is famous for one unusual feature: instead of feathered wings like other feathered dinosaurs, it had a skin membrane held out by a rod-like bone at the wrist, the same basic idea bats and flying squirrels use. No other known dinosaur has this wrist rod. It lived in the Middle to Late Jurassic, roughly 166 to 159 million years ago, and belongs to a small, poorly known family called scansoriopterygids - Yi qi is the only member of that family known to have this membrane wing.

Despite the bat-like wings, Yi qi was a true dinosaur, not a pterosaur and not a bat - both of those comparisons describe how the wing worked, not what the animal was. It was tiny: wingspan is commonly cited at about 60 cm, and its skull was under 4 cm long. Published weight estimates vary from roughly 0.38 to 0.52 kg across different sources, often rounded to under 0.4 kg - about the weight of a large pigeon. Its jaws carried a small number of forward-pointing teeth, and its short, deep skull suggests it hunted insects up in tree branches rather than on the ground, though the exact diet is inferred rather than directly preserved. Yi qi's wings work on a different principle from the feathered gliding wings on the four limbs of Microraptor - two very different dinosaur solutions to gliding or flying.

The fossil was found in the Tiaojishan Formation near Mutoudeng in Hebei Province, China, and was named and described in 2015 by Xu Xing, Zheng Xiaoting, Corwin Sullivan and colleagues, published in the journal Nature. The genus name Yi means "wing" and the species name qi means "strange" in Mandarin - together, roughly "strange wing".

Everything runs on your device with WebGL, so the model works without an account and without sending anything to a server. The skin tone and pattern are an artistic reconstruction, because fossils do not preserve color or soft tissue, and this model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton; the numbers above are real published values.
