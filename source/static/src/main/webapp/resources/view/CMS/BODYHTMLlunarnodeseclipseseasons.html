There is a New Moon every month, yet a solar eclipse only comes a couple of times a year. The reason is a tilt: the Moon orbit leans about 5 degrees to the ecliptic, the Sun apparent path, so most New Moons slip above or below the Sun. An eclipse needs the Moon near one of its two nodes - the points where the tilted orbit crosses the ecliptic - just as the Sun passes the same spot. Those windows, the eclipse seasons, arrive about every 173 days. Press play and watch the red zones light up.
