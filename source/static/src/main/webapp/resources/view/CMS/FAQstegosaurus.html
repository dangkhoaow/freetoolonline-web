Frequently Asked Questions

How big was a real Stegosaurus?

It varies by species and specimen. Most published estimates put length at about 6.5-9 m and weight at about 2.7-5.8 metric tons, with the largest known individuals near the top of those ranges. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the true scale against a 1.8 m person.

How many plates and spikes did it have?

Published estimates put the plate count at 17 to 22, arranged in two alternating rows from the neck to partway down the tail, with the largest plates - over the hips - exceeding 60 cm both wide and tall. The tail ended in four spikes, each roughly 60 to 90 cm long, likely sheathed in keratin in life.

What were the plates and spikes for?

Most researchers now agree the tail spikes were primarily a defensive weapon against predators. The back plates are thought to have served mainly for visual display - possibly for recognizing other Stegosaurus or for attracting mates - with a secondary, still-debated role in regulating body temperature.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The plate and spike proportions follow the fossil skeleton, but the skin color and texture are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values.

When and where did Stegosaurus live?

In the Late Jurassic, roughly 155 to 145 million years ago, with fossils found in the western United States (the Morrison Formation) and in Portugal. The genus was named in 1877 by Othniel Charles Marsh from a skeleton collected that year near Morrison, Colorado.
