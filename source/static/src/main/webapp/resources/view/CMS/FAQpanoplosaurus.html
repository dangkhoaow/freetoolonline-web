Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Panoplosaurus?

Published estimates put it at about 5 m (16 ft) long and roughly 1.6 tonnes (1,600 kg). Turn on the human figure in the viewer to compare scale against a 1.8 m person.

Did Panoplosaurus have a tail club like Ankylosaurus?

No. Panoplosaurus had no club at the end of its tail and lacked prominent spikes anywhere on its body, unlike the already-shipped Ankylosaurus and Pinacosaurus. It relied instead on dense, regularly shaped armor plates covering its neck and back.

When and where was it found?

Charles M. Sternberg found the first skeleton in 1917 at Quarry 8 near Little Sandhill Creek, Alberta, Canada. Lawrence M. Lambe formally named Panoplosaurus mirus in 1919, from the Dinosaur Park Formation, dated to about 76-75 million years ago.

What made Panoplosaurus unusual among ankylosaurs?

Its shoulder blade (scapulocoracoid) was completely fused, a feature not seen in other known ankylosaurs. Its broad, blunt-snouted skull carried lumpy bone ornamentation, and it was one of the last nodosaurids known before the group's decline at the end of the Cretaceous.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published length and mass estimates, but the exact armor plate pattern, skin color, and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not color or muscle shape. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
