Wendiceratops 3D Viewer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-21

This page renders a Wendiceratops as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click the frill, the nasal horn, or a hind leg to read a fact about that part.

Fossil hunter Wendy Sloboda found the bonebed in 2010 at the Pinhorn Provincial Grazing Reserve in southern Alberta, Canada. Paleontologists David C. Evans and Michael J. Ryan formally named and described Wendiceratops pinhornensis in 2015, combining Sloboda's name with the Greek "ceratops" (horned face). The bonebed sits in the lower Oldman Formation and dates to about 79 to 78.7 million years ago, in the Late Cretaceous - making it one of the oldest known large-horned centrosaurine ceratopsids, close to the base of the group that includes Triceratops.

Unlike Triceratops, Wendiceratops did not carry large brow horns. Instead it had a tall, erect horn on its nose, about 11.5 cm high with a 9 cm base, and a distinctive pair of frill spikes (the second and third epiparietals) that curve obliquely forward and upward into hooks rather than pointing straight back like most other centrosaurines. More than 200 bones have been recovered from the bonebed, including both adult and juvenile individuals.

Everything runs on your device with WebGL, so the model works without an account and without sending anything to a server. The skin tone and pattern are an artistic reconstruction, because fossils do not preserve color or soft tissue; this model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.
