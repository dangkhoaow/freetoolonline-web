Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Pinacosaurus?

Gregory Paul's systematic estimate puts a fully-grown adult at about 5 m long and 1.9 to 2.1 tonnes. Most known skeletons are smaller, younger individuals closer to 4 m and around 1 tonne, so cited sizes vary with the growth stage a source is describing. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the scale against a 1.8 m person.

Why does Pinacosaurus have extra holes around its nose?

Each nostril sits inside a group of several smaller openings - researchers have counted as many as five pairs in one juvenile skull. The exact purpose is still an open question: salt glands, a heat-exchange system for a dry habitat, and chambers for vocal resonance have all been proposed, but no single explanation has been confirmed.

Did Pinacosaurus have a tail club?

Yes, but a smaller one than Ankylosaurus carried, and it had not yet fully developed in the young animals found so far. Small rectangular armor plates called caputegulae also covered the skull, and the genus name Pinacosaurus means "plank lizard" after them.

When and where did Pinacosaurus live?

In the Late Cretaceous, about 75 to 71 million years ago, in what is now Mongolia and northern China. Walter Granger found the first fossils in 1923 at the Flaming Cliffs (Bayn Dzak) during the American Museum of Natural History's Central Asiatic Expeditions, and Charles Gilmore named the genus in 1933. More than 20 skeletons are now known - among the most of any armored dinosaur - and most are juveniles, including a group of five small individuals found together, suggesting young Pinacosaurus travelled in groups.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published figures, but the skin color and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values, with ranges cited because sources vary by growth stage and specimen.
