Impact Crater Formation 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

This browser explorer puts three real impact craters on one shared ground plane so you can compare their size directly, then drag an impactor-diameter slider to grow a fourth test crater and see roughly how big an impact of that size would leave behind.

Barringer Crater (Meteor Crater) in Arizona is about 1.19 km across and about 50,000 years old, dug out by an iron meteorite roughly 50 m wide. Chicxulub, buried under the Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico, is about 180 km across and about 66 million years old - the impact tied to the end-Cretaceous extinction. Vredefort Dome in South Africa is estimated at about 300 km across in its original form and about 2.02 billion years old, among the oldest and largest confirmed impact structures on Earth.

Because these three craters span from just over a kilometer to hundreds of kilometers, the scene draws every crater at a compressed (square-root) scale so the smallest stays visible next to the largest - the facts panel always lists the real, uncompressed diameters. The test crater uses a widely cited rule of thumb for simple bowl craters: final diameter is roughly 20 times the impactor's diameter, a scaling relationship that also depends on impact speed, angle, and the rock being hit.

  • Three real craters (Barringer, Chicxulub, Vredefort) rendered to a shared comparison scale
  • Click any crater rim for its real diameter, age, and impactor size
  • Impactor-diameter slider (5 m to 15 km) that regrows a test crater using the 20x scaling rule
  • Drag to orbit the camera, scroll or pinch to zoom
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

Students see why a 50 m rock left a 1.19 km scar while a 12 km rock left one 180 km wide; teachers use the slider to show how quickly crater size grows with impactor size; curious readers get a feel for how large the dinosaur-extinction impact really was next to the crater in their geography textbook.

This page teaches the general crater-formation scaling across Earth's own record. For a single named crater on another world, the collection also has close-up pages: Phobos-Stickney and Mimas-Herschel zoom into one giant crater each on a small moon, and Vesta-Rheasilvia covers the huge impact basin on the asteroid Vesta - each of those pages is about one specific feature, while this page is about the general size-versus-impactor relationship.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational approximation of crater scaling, not a physics solver - real crater size also depends on impact speed, angle, and target rock strength.
