Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Lambeosaurus?

Published estimates range from about 9 to 15 m long and about 4 to 6 tonnes. The wide range mostly reflects taxonomic revision history - several historically separate named species and growth-stage specimens are now regarded by modern taxonomy as growth stages or synonyms of fewer valid species, not one animal's variable size. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the scale against a 1.8 m person.

What made its crest different from other hadrosaurs?

Lambeosaurus had a hollow, hatchet or axe-shaped bony crest on top of its skull, formed by an extension of the nasal passages. It looks distinct from Parasaurolophus's long tube-shaped crest that curves back over the skull and from Saurolophus's solid, forward-pointing spike - three different crest shapes among hadrosaurs already on this site.

When and where did Lambeosaurus live?

In the Late Cretaceous, about 76 to 75 million years ago, in what is now Alberta, Canada. Canadian paleontologist William Parks named the genus in 1923, honoring fellow paleontologist Lawrence Lambe, from material collected in the Dinosaur Park Formation.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published figures, but the skin color and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values, with ranges cited because sources vary by named species and growth stage.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached. A free-licensed glTF model swaps in after first paint to show the real hatchet-shaped crest.
