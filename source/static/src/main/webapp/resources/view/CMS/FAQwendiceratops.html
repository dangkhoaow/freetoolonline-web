Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Wendiceratops?

Published estimates put it at about 6 m (20 ft) long and roughly 1.5 tonnes, with some sources citing a bit over 1 tonne. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to compare scale against a 1.8 m person.

Did Wendiceratops have brow horns like Triceratops?

No. Wendiceratops lacked large brow horns. Its most distinctive feature was a tall, erect nasal horn (about 11.5 cm high) plus a pair of frill spikes that curve forward into hooks, rather than pointing straight back as in most centrosaurines.

When and where was it found?

Fossil hunter Wendy Sloboda found the bonebed in 2010 at the Pinhorn Provincial Grazing Reserve in southern Alberta, Canada. David C. Evans and Michael J. Ryan formally named and described Wendiceratops pinhornensis in 2015, from the lower Oldman Formation, dated to about 79 to 78.7 million years ago.

Why is Wendiceratops important to ceratopsian evolution?

It is one of the oldest known large-horned centrosaurine ceratopsids, close to the base of the group that later includes Triceratops. Its hook-shaped frill spikes and prominent nasal horn help researchers trace how ceratopsid horn and frill ornamentation evolved over time.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published length and mass estimates, but frill shape, horn size, skin color, and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not color or muscle shape. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
