Frequently Asked Questions

How big was a real Styracosaurus?

Most published estimates put an adult at about 5-5.5 m long and roughly 1,800-2,700 kg, with a shoulder height around 1.8 m. Turn on the human figure in the viewer to see the true scale against a 1.8 m person.

How is Styracosaurus different from Triceratops?

Styracosaurus had a single large horn on its nose and no long brow horns, with a frill ringed by four to six long spikes. Triceratops instead had two long brow horns, a short nose horn, and a solid frill without long spikes. Both are ceratopsians from the Late Cretaceous of North America.

What were the frill spikes for?

Paleontologists think the spiked frill likely served for display and species recognition, and possibly for defense, similar to the reasoning applied to other horned ceratopsians. The frill and horn shapes come directly from the fossil skull.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The frill, spike, and horn proportions follow the fossil skeleton, but the skin color and texture are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not soft tissue or color. The length, weight, and age figures shown are real published values.

When and where did Styracosaurus live?

In the Late Cretaceous, roughly 76 to 75 million years ago, in what is now Alberta, Canada. The genus was named in 1913 by Lawrence Lambe, with its first fossils found the same year at what is now Dinosaur Provincial Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached.
