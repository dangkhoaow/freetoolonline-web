Some planetary rings are impossibly thin and sharp-edged, and for a while that was a puzzle: left to themselves, ring particles should shear and spread into a broad haze. The answer is shepherd moons - small satellites flanking a ring whose gravity herds the particles back into line. The inner moon nudges strays outward, the outer moon tugs them inward, and the ring stays a crisp ribbon. Press play, then toggle the shepherds off and watch the ring drift apart.

Published literacy: shepherd moons confine narrow rings by gravitational nudges - proposed in 1979 by Goldreich and Tremaine and confirmed by Voyager. Saturn thin F ring is flanked by Prometheus and Pandora (1980), while Uranus sharp epsilon ring is penned by Cordelia at 49,752 km and Ophelia at 53,764 km (1986).
