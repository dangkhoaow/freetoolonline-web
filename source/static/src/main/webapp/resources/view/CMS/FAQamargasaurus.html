Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Amargasaurus?

Published figures put Amargasaurus at about 9 to 13 m long and about 2.5 to 4 tonnes. This viewer uses 10 m and 3.2 t as mid-range values within those ranges. Turn on the human figure to compare scale against a 1.8 m person.

What are the neck spines for?

Fossils show two parallel rows of tall neural spines along the neck. Scientists debate whether they supported a sail-like skin membrane, stood as bony cores for keratin sheaths, or served another display or thermoregulatory role - the page treats that as an open question, not settled fact.

When and where was Amargasaurus found?

In the Early Cretaceous, about 130 to 120 million years ago, in the La Amarga Formation of Neuquen, Argentina. Leonardo Salgado and Jose Bonaparte named the genus in 1991 from holotype MACN-N 15.

How is Amargasaurus different from Diplodocus or Barosaurus?

All three are sauropods, but Amargasaurus was a dicraeosaurid - smaller, with a shorter neck than the long-necked diplodocids - and is defined by its twin rows of tall neck spines rather than an extremely elongated neck or whip tail.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published length and mass ranges, but the skin color, soft-tissue outline, and the exact shape of the neck spines are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not color or soft tissue. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. The 3D engine loads once and is then cached.
