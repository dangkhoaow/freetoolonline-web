Mimas Herschel Crater 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

Mimas is an inner icy moon of Saturn - about 396 kilometers across in Cassini summaries. On its leading hemisphere sits Herschel, an impact basin roughly 130 to 140 kilometers wide. That is nearly one third of the moon diameter, with floor depths often cited near 10 to 12 kilometers, walls about 5 kilometers high, and a central peak rising about 6 to 8 kilometers above the floor.

William Herschel discovered Mimas in 1789. Voyager 1 first returned a clear view of the crater in 1980; Cassini later flew close enough for stereo and high-resolution imaging, including a notable pass on 13 February 2010. Press coverage often notes a resemblance to the Death Star - a coincidence, because the crater was imaged years after the film. Some studies discuss a possible young ocean or elongated core; this page does not choose a side.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational visualization - the bowl, rim, and peak are simplified and exaggerated for visibility, not a Cassini digital elevation mesh, and not an impact hydrocode.
