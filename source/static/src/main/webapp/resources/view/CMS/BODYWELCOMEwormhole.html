Wormhole (Einstein-Rosen Bridge) 3D Explorer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-19

A wormhole throat with zero tidal force has an exact embedding shape: a catenoid, r(l) = b0*cosh(l/b0), where b0 is the throat radius and l is the proper radial distance measured out from the throat. Ludwig Flamm found the first version of this math in 1916 as a companion to Karl Schwarzschild's own solution. Albert Einstein and Nathan Rosen published the Einstein-Rosen bridge in 1935. Charles Misner and John Wheeler coined the word "wormhole" in 1957.

Two later results matter for what this page can honestly claim. In 1962, Robert Fuller and John Wheeler showed that a Schwarzschild wormhole pinches shut before even a beam of light can cross it - so that version is not traversable. In 1988, Michael Morris and Kip Thorne showed that holding a throat open for real travel needs exotic matter that violates the null energy condition, and no such matter has ever been observed. This explorer renders the catenoid geometry and states both results; it never claims a wormhole exists or that travel through one is possible.

  • Exact catenoid throat mesh built live from r(l) = b0*cosh(l/b0)
  • Throat-radius slider (b0) rescales the whole geometry in real time
  • Two flared mouths marked in orange and cyan
  • Illustrative traversal marker with an on/off toggle and a speed slider
  • Runs fully in the browser with the vendored three.js engine - no account, no upload

Anyone curious why science writers say "wormholes are allowed by the math but never seen" gets a concrete picture of the throat shape and the two results that explain the caveat.

Everything renders on your device with WebGL. The 3D engine loads once (about 0.7 MB) and is cached; no scene data is sent to a server.

This is an educational embedding diagram, not a solver for the full Einstein field equations and not a model of exotic-matter physics. The traversal marker is illustrative only - it does not represent a real crossing, since the non-traversable Schwarzschild case pinches shut first.
