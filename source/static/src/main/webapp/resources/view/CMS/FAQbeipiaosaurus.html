Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Beipiaosaurus important to paleontologists?

It is the species that gave the first direct fossil evidence of feathers anywhere in the therizinosaur family - long, simple, unbranched filaments preserved alongside its skeleton. Larger, later therizinosaurs such as Therizinosaurus have no feathers directly preserved on their own fossils, so their feathering is inferred from this smaller relative.

How big was Beipiaosaurus?

Published estimates put it at roughly 1.8 to 2.6 m long, with this page using 2.2 m. Weight is much less certain - estimates range from about 27 kg up to roughly 135 kg depending on the method used, so this page cites 85 kg as a commonly used mid-range figure. Was Beipiaosaurus a plant eater?

Probably, yes. It belongs to the therizinosaurs, a group of theropod dinosaurs that mostly evolved toward slow-moving, herbivorous lifestyles despite descending from meat-eating ancestors. Beipiaosaurus is one of the earliest and smallest known members of that group.

Did Beipiaosaurus have big claws like Therizinosaurus?

No - its forelimbs were elongated but only modestly clawed, closer to earlier theropod ancestors than to the huge, sickle-shaped claws later therizinosaurs such as Therizinosaurus evolved. The enlarged claws seen in that later relative developed further along the family's evolution.

When and where did Beipiaosaurus live?

In the Early Cretaceous, roughly 125 million years ago, in what is now the Yixian Formation of Liaoning Province, China - one of the most productive fossil beds for feathered dinosaurs. It was named by Xu, Tang and Wang in 1999 from a holotype specimen (IVPP V11559).

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The body proportions follow published length and mass figures for this species, but the skin color and pattern are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone and, for this species, feather filaments, but not soft-tissue color.
