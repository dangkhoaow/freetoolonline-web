Frequently Asked Questions

How big was Chasmosaurus?

Published length estimates commonly fall around 4.3 to 5 m. Mass estimates span about 1.5 to 2.5 tonnes depending on the reconstruction; this viewer uses 4.8 m and 2 t as mid-range panel values within those ranges. Turn on the human figure to compare scale against a 1.8 m person.

What are the openings in the frill?

Chasmosaurus is known for a large, fenestrated frill - bony openings (fenestrae) in the shield behind the skull. In life those windows would have been covered by skin and possibly used for display, species recognition, or thermoregulation; the exact soft-tissue look is not preserved in fossils.

How is Chasmosaurus different from Triceratops?

Both are ceratopsians, but Chasmosaurus is an earlier chasmosaurine with a longer, more fenestrated frill and relatively smaller brow horns. Triceratops lived later, had a shorter solid frill, and much longer brow horns. This site has separate viewers for each species.

When and where was it found?

In the Late Cretaceous, about 76.5 to 75.5 million years ago, in the Dinosaur Park Formation of Alberta, Canada. Lawrence Lambe named the genus in 1914 from material collected in that region.

Is the model scientifically accurate?

The proportions follow published length and mass ranges, but frill shape, horn size, skin color, and soft-tissue outline are an artistic reconstruction - fossils preserve bone, not color or muscle shape. This model is not a fossil-accurate skeleton.

Do I need to install anything to view it?

No. The model renders in your browser with WebGL - no app, no account, and nothing about your visit is sent to a server. When available, a free-licensed glTF model may swap in after first paint.
