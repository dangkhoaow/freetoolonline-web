Dunkleosteus 3D Viewer

Last reviewed: 2026-07-23

This page renders Dunkleosteus terrelli as a 3D model you can spin in the browser - drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, toggle a 1.8 m person beside it for scale, and click its head, body, or tail to read a fact about that part. Dunkleosteus was an armored fish, not a dinosaur.

Dunkleosteus terrelli lived roughly 382 to 358 million years ago, during the Late Devonian period - tens of millions of years before the first dinosaurs existed. Instead of true teeth, its jaw was made of sharp bony plates that formed self-sharpening, blade-like edges strong enough to crush the armor of other fish; a 2006 biomechanical study estimated a bite force around 7,400 newtons at the jaw tip, in the same range as Tyrannosaurus rex and modern crocodiles. It is thought to have eaten other fish, cephalopods, and even other placoderms, swallowing prey whole and regurgitating the indigestible parts.

Its size is genuinely disputed among paleontologists. Only the head and chest armor of Dunkleosteus commonly survive as fossils, so the rest of the body has to be estimated by comparison with other fish. For decades, popular sources cited lengths up to about 8-10 m and weights near 4 tonnes. A 2023 statistical study by Russell Engelman (Case Western Reserve University, published in the journal Diversity) used a new measurement method across thousands of fish specimens and suggested much smaller individuals, roughly 3.4 to 4.1 m long - a conclusion other researchers have since challenged, so this page shows the smaller, more recent figure while noting the older estimate is still widely repeated. The first fossils were found in 1867 near Sheffield Lake, Ohio, on the shore of Lake Erie; the genus was named in 1956 by paleontologist David Dunkle, honoring both Dunkle and the amateur collector who found it, Jay Terrell.
